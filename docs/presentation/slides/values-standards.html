What Makes a Persona Powerful

Philosophy & Thought Patterns

An Operating Philosophy teaches the model how to think, not just what to do.

Novelty Over Familiarity:
"Would this appear on the first page of a search engine? If yes, dig deeper." › This single principle turned a generic carbonara into the zucchini variation.
Garden First:
"Prioritize fresh herbs from the household garden." › The model now defaults to ingredients you actually have.
The Tinkerer's Mindset:
"Always include variations, substitutions, and creative twists." › Every recipe comes with ideas for experimentation.

A model with such guidance makes better judgment calls on things you didn't anticipate.

This is the difference between a rule and a value. Rules handle known situations — "use metric units." Values handle unknown situations — when the model faces a choice you didn't foresee, the philosophy guides the decision. "Novelty Over Familiarity" is a great example: you can't list every obvious recipe to avoid, but you can teach the model to always look past the obvious. These principles are from the actual Recipe Curator persona. You can apply the same approach to any domain — a travel planner, a writing assistant, a research helper. The equipment table on the previous slide showed domain knowledge — situational awareness. This slide shows values — quality standards. Together, they let the model make judgment calls instead of just following commands.
